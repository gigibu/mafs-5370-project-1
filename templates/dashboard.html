Summary

How the RL solver works, why it outperforms the naive Merton benchmark, and what the four cases demonstrate.

THE PROBLEM
An investor starts with wealth W₀ = 1 and must decide, at each of T discrete time steps, how much of their wealth to place in risky assets versus a risk-free account earning 2% per period. The objective is to maximise the expected utility of terminal wealth, E[U(WT)], where utility captures the investor's degree of risk aversion via the CRRA parameter γ. The critical constraint is a rebalancing limit of 10 percentage points per asset per period: the investor cannot shift more than 10 pp in or out of any single asset in one step. This constraint, inspired by transaction-cost and broker-throttle models, is what makes the problem non-trivial and is the source of the performance gap between RL and the naive benchmark.
✓ RL: Backward Induction
1
Start at the end of the horizon (t = T). The value of any portfolio state at that final moment is simply its utility: Q(s, a) = U(WT). No guessing needed — this is an exact boundary condition.
2
Work backwards, one period at a time. At each step t, fit a Q-value approximator that maps every (state, action) pair to the expected total future utility, using the values computed at t+1 as targets. This gives the solver full look-ahead over the entire remaining horizon.
3
Extract the greedy policy at t = 0. At every time step, pick the feasible allocation — one that respects both the 10 pp limit and no-leverage constraint — that maximises the Q-value. Crucially, the solver evaluates all reachable actions, not just the Merton target.
✗ Naive Merton Benchmark
1
Compute the unconstrained optimal fraction. The Merton (1969) formula gives the theoretically ideal portfolio share: π* = (μ − r) / (γσ²). For example, with γ=2, μ=6%, σ=20%, r=2%, this is π* = 0.04 / (2×0.04) = 50%.
2
Apply a binary feasibility gate. Check whether the required trade fits within the 10 pp limit. If yes, execute it. If no — regardless of how close the target is — do nothing at all. This all-or-nothing rule is the fundamental flaw.
3
Result: the investor holds cash for most configurations. Starting from θ₀=0, the required trade equals π* itself. Since π*>10% for γ=1,2,5, the gate blocks the trade every single period. The portfolio never earns any risky premium.
WHAT THE FOUR CASES DEMONSTRATE
Cases 1 and 2 (T=1) isolate the binary-gate failure in its simplest form. In a single period, RL immediately moves to θ=0.10 — the best reachable allocation — while Naive Merton does nothing (γ=1,2,5). Case 2 shows the same failure repeating independently across all four assets simultaneously, amplifying the utility gap.

Cases 3 and 4 (T=10) introduce the ramp-up phenomenon. RL backward induction discovers that the optimal multi-period strategy is to move 10 pp closer to π* every period, eventually converging to the full Merton allocation. Naive Merton, re-evaluating from θ_old=0 at every step, is blocked every single period and never invests. Case 4 compounds this failure across both time and assets simultaneously.

In all cases, the fix requires no new mathematics: a projected Merton policy that clips the unconstrained π* to the nearest feasible allocation achieves exactly the same outcome as RL. The gap is entirely a consequence of the naive implementation's all-or-nothing gate.

🏆 Key Finding

The performance advantage of RL is not a fundamental limitation of the Merton framework. Merton's formula correctly identifies the optimal unconstrained target. The problem is purely in how the benchmark implements feasibility: it treats a blocked trade as a reason to hold cash, when the correct response is to move as close to the target as constraints allow.

RL backward induction discovers this naturally, because it evaluates all feasible actions — not just the Merton target. The resulting policy is a constrained version of the Merton strategy, and it dominates the naive implementation across every γ, every time horizon, and every number of assets tested.
